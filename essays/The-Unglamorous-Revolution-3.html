All these mouths to feed and not enough food? Haber-Bosch process is here to save the world!

I jest but they created arguably the single most important chemical process that has kept humanity out of the perils of mass starvation.

Shree Solanki  ·  12 June 2025  ·  7 min read

The Process That Feeds Half the World

The Unglamorous Revolution, No. 3 of 10

In 1909, Fritz Haber demonstrated that nitrogen could be pulled from the air and converted into ammonia using high pressure, high temperature, and an iron catalyst. In 1913, Carl Bosch scaled this process to industrial production at a plant in Oppau, Germany.

Between them, they solved the most important problem in the history of agriculture: how to feed a growing population on a finite amount of land.

Roughly half the nitrogen atoms in your body right now entered the biosphere through the Haber-Bosch process. You are, in a meaningful chemical sense, partly a product of an industrial reaction invented in Germany in the early 20th century. So are approximately 4 billion other people who would not otherwise exist.

This is either a remarkable triumph of human ingenuity or a deeply uncomfortable fact about how dependent modern civilisation is on a single industrial process, depending on how you prefer to spend your existential dread.

Why Nitrogen

All living things need nitrogen. It is essential for building proteins and DNA. The atmosphere is 78% nitrogen. Plants and animals cannot use atmospheric nitrogen directly. It exists as N2, two nitrogen atoms bound together with a triple bond, which is one of the strongest in chemistry.

For most of human history, the nitrogen available for agriculture came from a narrow set of sources: animal manure, human waste, crop rotation with legumes (whose root bacteria can fix nitrogen), and guano mined from Pacific islands where seabirds had been depositing nitrogen-rich droppings for centuries.

These sources were finite and, in the case of guano, geographically constrained. By the late 19th century, population growth had made the nitrogen constraint existential. Yields on agricultural land were flattening. The philosopher and economist William Crookes gave a lecture to the British Association in 1898 that was essentially a well-sourced prediction of mass starvation. The world needed a new nitrogen source. The chemistry to produce one existed in theory. The engineering to produce it at scale did not.

What Haber and Bosch Actually Did

The chemistry is: N2 + 3H2 -> 2NH3. Nitrogen plus hydrogen becomes ammonia. The ammonia is then used to make fertiliser.

The problem is that the reaction is extremely difficult to drive at scale. It requires temperatures of 400-500 degrees Celsius, pressures of 150-300 atmospheres (roughly 150-300 times atmospheric pressure at sea level), and a catalyst to make it proceed at a useful rate. In 1909, building equipment that could reliably maintain these conditions without exploding was not trivial. Carl Bosch's contribution was, in large part, solving the engineering problem of building pressure vessels that would not fail.

The BASF plant at Oppau, which opened in 1913, was, by the standards of its time, an extraordinary piece of industrial engineering. It produced ammonia continuously at a scale that made synthetic fertiliser economically viable for the first time. Within a decade, the technology had spread to other countries, and the era of synthetic nitrogen fertiliser had begun.

The Population Consequence

The relationship between nitrogen fertiliser and population is among the most direct causal links in modern history.

In 1900, the world had 1.6 billion people. It was already straining against the limits of what existing agricultural methods could support. By 2023, the world had 8 billion people. The land under cultivation had expanded substantially, but not by a factor of five. What changed was yield (basically, the amount of food produced per unit of land), and nitrogen fertiliser is the single largest driver of that yield increase.

A 2008 study by the researcher Vaclav Smil estimated that the Haber-Bosch process is responsible for feeding roughly 40% of the current world population. Other estimates run higher. The precise number depends on your modelling assumptions, but no serious analyst disputes that without synthetic nitrogen fertiliser, global food production would be dramatically lower than it is, and the population the planet could support would be correspondingly smaller.

The arithmetic is uncomfortable but not complicated: if 40 to 50% of current food production depends on synthetic nitrogen, and you removed that production, somewhere between 3 and 4 billion people would face starvation. Not inconvenience. Starvation. The Haber-Bosch process is not a chapter in agricultural history. It is the reason most people alive today are alive.

The Part Nobody Likes to Mention

Fritz Haber won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1918. He accepted it the year after his wife, also a chemist, shot herself in the garden of their home. Clara Immerwahr Haber had allegedly opposed her husband's work on chemical weapons (he was the leading figure in Germany's chlorine and mustard gas programs in World War I) and had spent years arguing that using chemistry to kill people was a perversion of science. The night of her death, Haber left for the Eastern Front to oversee another gas attack. He was, by any measure, both the man who enabled the feeding of billions and the man who weaponised industrial chemistry for mass killing.

The same nitrogen chemistry that makes fertiliser makes explosives. Ammonia can be converted to nitric acid, which can be combined with various things to make things that explode. The Haber-Bosch process enabled German munitions production in World War I, extending the war that might otherwise have ended earlier due to Germany's inability to produce enough explosives. The process that saved billions of lives also, very probably, prolonged a war that killed millions.

This is the part that does not fit neatly into the triumph narrative.

The Modern Problem

Nitrogen fertiliser has not only enabled population growth. It has also, in the quantities now used globally, become a significant environmental problem.

Plants absorb only a fraction of the nitrogen applied to agricultural fields. The rest runs off into waterways, where it causes eutrophication (yay Mom, I learned a new word!), algal blooms that deplete oxygen, kill fish, and create dead zones. The Gulf of Mexico dead zone, fed by nitrogen runoff from the Mississippi River basin, covers an area roughly the size of New Jersey. Nitrogen oxides released from agricultural soils are a significant contributor to greenhouse gas emissions. The nitrogen cycle, which human activity has now transformed more radically than at any point in the past 2.5 billion years, is under pressure at a global scale.

We needed more nitrogen than the natural cycle could provide. We engineered a way to get it. We used it on a scale that has now perturbed the natural cycle itself. This is the pattern of transformative technology: the solution to one constraint creates the next constraint, at a larger scale.

The Unglamorous Point

The Haber-Bosch process is, by one defensible measure, the most important technological achievement in human history. Nothing else has as direct a line to the survival of as many people. It is also a story about the inseparability of creation and destruction, about the same chemistry that feeds children being the chemistry that fills shells, and about what happens when you solve a problem at a scale that the natural world was not designed to absorb.

Fritz Haber died in 1934, in exile, his German citizenship stripped by the Nazi government whose rise he had not opposed early enough, his Jewish identity now the only thing that mattered to the state he had served. The man who fed the world died fleeing a country that was about to attempt to murder most of the people he shared a heritage with, including relatives who would be killed in concentration chambers that used a pesticide his colleagues had developed.

Chemistry is morally neutral. The uses of chemistry are not.
