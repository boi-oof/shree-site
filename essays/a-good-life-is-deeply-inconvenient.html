A Good Life Is Deeply Inconvenient

My Father Knew This. I Spent Years Proving Him Wrong. He Was Right.

Shree Solanki 12 June 2024 8 min read

There is a certain kind of person who will tell you, with great conviction, that they have optimized their life. They have removed friction. They have automated the annoying parts. They have, through a combination of apps and habits and a firm policy of never doing anything the hard way, constructed an existence that runs smoothly, efficiently, and (though they would not use this word) emptily.

I was that person. And the years I spent being that person were, not coincidentally, the years I made the least progress, felt the least alive, and grew, in every meaningful sense, the least.

It took me an embarrassingly long time to understand something that my father, a man who seemed to treat inconvenience as a personal hobby, had been demonstrating my entire life without ever explaining it. A good life is not a comfortable one. It is, in fact, reliably, structurally, almost defiantly uncomfortable. And the discomfort is not the price you pay for the good parts. The discomfort is the good parts.

The Friction Removal Project

At some point convenience became the supreme value of my life. Not happiness. Not meaning. Not love or purpose or the kind of deep satisfaction that comes from doing something genuinely hard. Convenience. The minimization of effort. The shortest path between wanting something and having it.

We built entire industries around this. Not just delivery apps and one-click purchases, but a whole philosophy of self-management organized around the question: how do I make this easier? How do I remove the step I don't want to do? How do I get the outcome without the process?

And the answer, it turned out (to my utter disappointment), was: you can't. Not really. I mean you certainly can but not in any way that makes any sense in the long term.

Because the process is not the obstacle to the outcome. The process is the point. The effort is not what you pay to get the thing. Nope. Effort is the thing, transformed into capability, into understanding, into the kind of person who can do the thing next time without needing to be dragged to it. Strip the effort out and you don't get a better outcome. You get the same outcome, minus yourself.

I know this because I tried very hard, for a very long time, to get outcomes without processes, and what I got was a life that looked approximately fine from the outside and felt, from the inside, like I was grasping at any semblance of meaning. (I consumed so much self help content in this period and all it did was delude me into thinking that this excessive obsession with automating my whole life is in fact the solution.)

What Inconvenience Actually Is

My father understood this in his bones in the way people who grew up without the luxury of convenience understand it — not as philosophy but as simple fact. He was inconvenienced constantly and he treated each inconvenience with a kind of baffling cheerful aggression, as if it had personally challenged him and he intended to win. He fixed things himself that could have been fixed by someone else, not because he couldn't afford otherwise but because knowing how to fix things mattered to him. He helped people when it was expensive to do so. Expensive in time, in effort, in the specific currency of a Saturday morning that could have been spent doing nothing. He was, in this way, relentlessly unavailable for comfort.

I spent most of my youth finding this baffling. Why make it harder? Why not just, y'know, not? Why volunteer for the friction when the friction-free option was, quite literally, right there?

And then I grew up and tried the friction-free option and found out.

The Grief of Not Growing

Here is what nobody tells you about the careful avoidance of inconvenience: it works so well. That is the trap. In the short term, it is entirely, genuinely effective. You do not have the difficult conversation, and the difficult moment passes. You do not attempt the hard thing, and you do not fail at the hard thing. You do not put yourself in situations that might be uncomfortable, and you are, measurably, less uncomfortable.

The bill arrives later. It always arrives later. And it arrives not as a single catastrophic event but as a slow, creeping, awful realization: you are exactly the same person you were three years ago. Five years ago. You have not changed because you did not let anything change you. You protected yourself so comprehensively from the experiences that shape people that you arrived at your current life so unshapen that everyone can see you are soft in the middle, uncertain at the edges, faintly confused about who you actually are and what you're actually for.

That grief of your potential quietly expiring, of looking around and understanding that you have been very comfortable and very stuck is a particular kind of grief because it has no clean cause. Nothing happened to you. You just didn't let enough things happen to you, and here you are.

The Thing About Service

At some point (and I think this is the turn, the thing that actually changed the direction of things) I stumbled across an understanding about what I actually find meaningful, which is other people.

Not in a greeting-card sense. Not in the sense of vague goodwill and a concern for humanity in general. In the specific, effortful, frequently inconvenient sense of showing up for actual humans in their actual circumstances. Being useful. Being present. Doing the thing that is needed rather than the thing that is easy.

This is where the inconvenience argument stops being abstract and becomes very concrete, because there is no such thing as genuine service that isn't inconvenient. Service, by definition, is the reorientation of your comfort around someone else's need. It requires the thing you were doing to matter less than the thing they need. It requires availability when unavailability would have been easier. It requires the long phone call, the drive across town, the held door, the hard truth told kindly, the problem engaged with rather than diplomatically admired from a safe distance.

And here is the strange alchemy of it: the more genuinely inconvenient the service, the more it fills something. Not in a martyrdom sense becuase suffering for its own sake is just suffering, and anyone who tells you otherwise is trying to sell you something. But in the sense that doing hard things for people you care about is one of the few activities that produces real satisfaction rather than the thin, quickly-evaporating simulation of it that convenience tends to offer.

Purpose, it turns out, has very low tolerance for comfort. It lives almost exclusively in the inconvenient places. So when you work for the problem you didn't have to engage with but did, when you showed up for the person you didn't have to show up for, you found within yourself a new version that got created by choosing the harder option enough times that it stopped feeling like a hard choice and started feeling like your character.

The Inheritance I Didn't Want and Eventually Needed

I have come around, late and somewhat sheepishly, to my father's view.

Not fully though, I draw the line at voluntarily making things difficult for the sake of difficulty. There is no virtue in refusing a working escalator when you have somewhere to be. I am not arguing for inconvenience as performative theatre to prove something to the world, or for the specific joy some people seem to take in doing things the hard way because it makes them feel hardy.

I am arguing for the willingness to be inconvenienced. The refusal to make the avoidance of difficulty the primary operating principle of your life. The understanding that friction is not your enemy and that it is, in fact, the thing that determines the shape of you, the way wind determines the shape of a tree, and that a life without it produces roughly the same result as a tree grown indoors: alive, technically, but noticeably lacking something.

My father didn't teach me this by lecturing me. He taught me by example, by the specific, observable fact of him moving toward difficulty rather than away from it, year after year, with what I now recognise as something very close to joy.

I thought he was wrong for a long time. The evidence suggested otherwise and I ignored it. That is also a lesson, I suppose - about the inconvenience of being forced to admit that the person who seemed most out of step with the modern world was, in fact, most in step with the only things that actually matter. He was right. It's deeply annoying. I'm grateful for it.

A good life is not the one with the fewest problems. It is the one where you became, problem by problem, someone worth being.
