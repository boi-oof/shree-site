The Switch That Runs Everything

The Unglamorous Revolution, No. 4 of 10

Preface - I, personally, would never think that transistors are unglamorous. They are absolute icons and divas. But I do believe that the general population isn't aware enough about them to know their aura. So without further ado.

On December 23, 1947, three physicists at Bell Laboratories in Murray Hill, New Jersey, demonstrated a device that amplified an electrical signal using a piece of germanium and two gold contacts. They called it a transistor. Their supervisor, who understood what had just happened, wanted to announce it to the world immediately. The management of Bell Labs, which was then the research division of AT&T, made them wait six months.

This seems, in retrospect, like the most significant failure of urgency in corporate history.

The transistor is the fundamental building block of all modern electronics. Every computer, every smartphone, every server, every digital camera, every pacemaker, every microwave oven, every car built after 1980, every piece of medical imaging equipment, every satellite, and the entire infrastructure of the internet is, at its foundation, made of transistors. A modern processor contains tens of billions of them. They switch on and off billions of times per second. They are smaller than a virus.

Everything you do that involves a digital device involves transistors. Most things you do that do not obviously involve a digital device also involve transistors. The transistor is the atom of the information age.

What a Transistor Does

A transistor is a switch. That is the entire job. It is on, or it is off. Current flows or it does not. One or zero.

The genius is not in the individual switch. The genius is in what you can build when you have billions of switches that you can arrange in logic gates (AND, OR, NOT) that, in combination, can perform any computation. The transistor did not replace the vacuum tube because it was conceptually different. It replaced it because it was smaller, faster, more reliable, used less power, and generated less heat.

The vacuum tube had been the active component in electronics since the early 20th century. It worked. It was just large, fragile, power-hungry, and had a habit of burning out. ENIAC, one of the first general-purpose computers, built in 1945, used 18,000 vacuum tubes, consumed 150 kilowatts of power, and occupied 1,800 square feet. It broke down approximately once every two days.

A transistor does the same job as a vacuum tube in a package that is, today, measured in nanometres. The current generation of processors uses transistors with features as small as 3 nanometres. A human hair is approximately 80,000 nanometres wide. You could fit 26,000 of these transistors across the width of a human hair.

Moore's Law and the Compounding

In 1965, Gordon Moore, one of the founders of Intel, observed that the number of transistors on an integrated circuit had roughly doubled every two years since the transistor's invention, and predicted this trend would continue.

Moore's Law, as it became known, is not a physical law. It is an observation that became a target that became a self-fulfilling prophecy. The semiconductor industry organised itself around it. Companies invested in the R&D required to sustain doubling. Customers made purchasing decisions based on the assumption that next year's chip would be twice as capable as this year's. The prediction became a coordination mechanism.

For roughly 50 years, it held. The consequences of sustained exponential growth in computing power are difficult to fully grasp because human beings are not good at intuiting exponential curves. A useful framing: if cars had improved at the rate that transistors improved between 1971 and 2021, a car today would travel at 420,000 miles per hour, cost less than a cent, and fit in your hand.

This rate of improvement is why your phone is more powerful than a 1990s supercomputer. It is why machine learning became practical in the 2010s - not because the algorithms improved dramatically, but because the hardware became cheap enough and fast enough to run them at scale. This is why the cost of sequencing a human genome dropped from $3 billion in 2003 to under $1,000 today. Nearly every technological development of the past 70 years is, in some part, a downstream consequence of the sustained miniaturisation of the transistor.

The Geopolitics of Sand

Transistors are made from silicon, refined from sand, in a manufacturing process of extraordinary complexity called semiconductor fabrication. A modern chip fabrication plant (fab, which I will certainly be researching and writing about) costs between $15 and $20 billion to build, takes three to five years to construct, and requires equipment so precise that the machines used to etch circuit patterns on silicon are themselves the most complex manufactured objects in human history.

There are currently two companies in the world capable of manufacturing the most advanced semiconductors: TSMC in Taiwan and Samsung in South Korea. Intel is attempting to rejoin this tier. One company, ASML, a Dutch firm, makes the extreme ultraviolet lithography machines without which advanced chips cannot be made. ASML has a global monopoly on this technology and produces approximately 50 of these machines per year, each costing $150 million, each weighing 180 tonnes, each shipped in 40 cargo containers.

The world's most powerful computing devices depend on a supply chain that runs through one company in the Netherlands, one island in the Pacific, and one peninsula in Northeast Asia. Taiwan produces approximately 90% of the world's most advanced chips. China has stated, with increasing clarity, that it regards Taiwan as part of China. The United States has passed legislation (CHIPS Act) specifically to rebuild domestic semiconductor manufacturing capacity. The transistor, which started as a physics experiment in New Jersey, has become the central object of great power competition in the 21st century.

The Unglamorous Point

The transistor is on the list of things you interact with every second of your waking life and think about approximately never. The phone in your pocket contains more transistors than there are stars in the Milky Way. The laptop running your spreadsheets contains a processor switching between states 3 billion times per second. The MRI machine that found someone's cancer, the GPS system that stopped you from driving into a lake, the server running the search engine you use to settle arguments: all transistors.

The three physicists who demonstrated the device in December 1947 - John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley - won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1956. Shockley went on to found a semiconductor company in California that failed commercially but spawned, through the defection of its engineers, the companies that became the American semiconductor industry, including Fairchild Semiconductor and Intel. The area where they settled is called Silicon Valley. It is named after the material transistors are made from.

The world's most valuable industry traces its physical and intellectual lineage to a piece of germanium and two gold contacts in a New Jersey laboratory. That is, by any reasonable measure, a reasonable return on a physics experiment.
