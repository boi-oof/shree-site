PSA Mums : Sabrina Carpenter is fine to listen to if your teen wants to, pinky promise!

Raunchy songs ask you to watch. Demure songs ask you to become. Caution might apply more where you think it is least needed.

Shree Solanki  ·  02 March 2025  ·  5 min read

The Demure Girl Song Is More Dangerous Than the Raunchy One

I have a jhula on my porch that faces the garden. This summer I have spent a lot of time on it, and from it I have watched a group of sixth-grade girls navigate each other. The politics are extraordinarily complex for people who are, at most, twelve. Who sits next to whom. Who gets ignored in a way that is technically deniable. Who laughs a little too hard at a joke to stay inside the circle. I watched all of this with the detached fascination of someone who has already survived it and the mild horror of someone who remembers exactly what it felt like.

At some point, one of them started playing music on a phone. I didn't catch the song. But I caught the dynamic around it: who knew the words, who pretended to, who used knowing them as a small credential, a proof of belonging.

This is not new. In my time, it was Taylor Swift versus Miley Cyrus. Pick one. The choice said something about you, and everyone knew what it said. Swift was the girl who wrote letters and waited and felt everything with her whole chest. Cyrus was the girl who burned it all down and danced in the ashes and did not apologise. You could not, in the cafeteria economy of the sixth grade, be genuinely neutral. You belong with me or Can't be tamed. Soft or wild. One orbit or the other.

Now, from what I can tell, the poles have shifted. On one side: Sabrina Carpenter, Chappell Roan, Charli XCX. Songs with a wink in them, a self that knows exactly what it is doing. Carpenter's Espresso is a song sung by someone who is completely comfortable with her own power and finds the whole thing a little funny. Roan's Good Luck, Babe is a woman who knows she is right and will wait for you to figure it out. These songs have protagonists.

On the other side is what I can only call the demure girl genre. Gracie Abrams singing about a boy's new girlfriend and wondering if he talks about her the way he used to talk about you. Olivia Rodrigo cataloguing the specific torture of watching someone move on. Tate McRae asking, in various configurations, why she is not enough. Soft vocals, minor keys, production that sounds like lying on the floor of your bedroom at 2am, lyrics that orbit a boy's attention like a satellite that has forgotten it had its own trajectory.

Here is my problem. And I want to be precise about it, because this is not a screed against any particular artist. Heartbreak is real. Longing is real. The desire to be wanted by someone who is not paying attention is one of the oldest feelings in the human catalogue, and it deserves its songs.

My problem is with what happens when a twelve-year-old makes that sound her personality.

The raunchy pop song has always made adults nervous. It is too loud, too sexual, too much. But the raunchy pop song is, in a way, honest about what it is. It is a performance. It is excess. It has a wink in it. Even the most provocative pop star is playing a character you can see the seams of. Cyrus doing Wrecking Ball was not asking you to become anything. She was asking you to watch.

The demure girl song asks you to become something. It asks you to be small, and to frame smallness as sensitivity. To be patient, and to frame patience as depth. To need very little, visibly, so that the right person will notice how little you need and reward you for it. It sells waiting as a personality. It makes a virtue out of not asking for too much.

For an adult, this is fine. I am in my early twenties and settled into the comfortable lulls of my own life, and if I want to lie on my floor and listen to Gracie Abrams wonder whether she is being thought about, I will do that on a Tuesday evening and feel my feelings and move right on. I have enough selfhood to borrow an emotion from a song without it colonising my entire sense of what I am supposed to want.

A sixth grader does not have that yet. A sixth grader is still building the architecture of herself, and she is building it out of whatever materials are available: what her friends say, what gets her included, and what the music she listens to tells her about what a girl is supposed to be.

You belong with me had a self in it. A girl who wanted something, said she wanted it (no matter how problematically), and believed she deserved it. You could argue with Swift's version of romance. But there was at least someone there doing the arguing by having an opinion.

The demure girl song, at its softest and most viral, offers something else entirely: a girl so focused on whether she is wanted that the question of what she wants has quietly disappeared. That is not sensitivity. That is erasure with good production.

Girlhood at twelve is brutal precisely because belonging feels like survival. The clique is not a social preference; it is a lifeline. And the music you like is not just music. It is a signal, a membership card, a performance of the kind of girl you are trying to be.

When the music that signals belonging is music about shrinking, about waiting, about measuring your worth in someone else's attention, that is not a harmless aesthetic preference. It is a twelve-year-old rehearsing, over and over, a way of being in the world that will take years to unlearn.

I am not scared of the raunchy song. I am not even scared of the heartbreak song, because heartbreak is honest. I am scared of the song that peddles the clean, demure girl aesthetic - the one with the soft vocals and the pretty production and the lyric that is, underneath everything, just a girl asking to be chosen by a boy, by becoming so small that it's no effort to choose you.

That one is quieter, and what it does to a young girl's temperament is very hard to undo even years later. That is why it worries me.
