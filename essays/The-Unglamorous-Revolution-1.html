AC chalu karo, bhai, please!

I walked into my plant in 43 degrees and cried tears of joy on entering my air conditioned plant. Here is how the AC shaped the world's economy.

Shree Solanki  ·  27 May 2025  ·  8 min read

How Air Conditioning Runs the World

Preface to this series: I work on a car-and-battery assembly line in Gujarat. The Gujarati word "loo" makes me shake in revolt and fear in a way that is much worse than what your English comprehension of the word brings up. It basically means the hot and sandy winds that nauseate you, give you fevers, and suck the life out of you. Working in such environments is not for the faint of heart, especially when the aforementioned work is in an assembly shop. Lucky for me, being moved to the battery division meant I worked in an air-conditioned plant. Dearest Reader, there is nothing in the world of battery supply chains that does not require sensitive temperature control. Which got me thinking - there is nothing in the world that can be made productively without efficient control of temperature. Which brings me to count all the boring stuff that powers the world. Now, I am an electrical engineer mainly because I was terrible at thermodynamics, so it is a bold choice to be writing my first essay by researching the AC. But here we go. Without much further ado.

The Unglamorous Revolution, No. 1 of 10

In 1902, a young engineer named Willis Carrier solved a humidity problem at a printing plant in Brooklyn. The ink was smearing because the air was too wet. His solution was a machine that cooled and dehumidified the air. He didn't intend to change the world. It was intended to stop ink from smearing.

But it did change the world.

We talk about the Industrial Revolution as if it were powered entirely by coal and iron and the Protestant work ethic of showing up and getting the work done. We talk about the rise of the American Sun Belt as if it were only the result of entrepreneurial spirit and good governance. We talk about the modernisation of Southeast Asia, the Gulf States, and the Indian subcontinent as triumphs of economic policy. In almost none of these conversations do we mention the machine sitting in the corner, humming quietly, keeping everything and everyone from melting.

This is the story of that machine.

The Problem With Heat

Before we get to the solution, it is worth sitting with the problem. Heat is not merely uncomfortable. Heat is cognitively and physically debilitating in ways that compound across populations and across time.

A study published in the Quarterly Journal of Economics in 2012 found that a single day above 29 degrees Celsius reduces that year's economic output measurably. Not dramatically, but measurably. Across a summer, across a decade, the effect accumulates. This is not a study of people lying on the floor fanning themselves. It is a study of what happens to productivity, decision-making, and output when temperatures rise.

Now extend this backward. Before air conditioning, the entire American South was economically marginal. Not because Southerners were less capable or less hardworking, but because running a factory, a textile mill, a steel operation, or a financial institution in 38-degree heat with 90 percent humidity is genuinely difficult. Workers tire faster. Mistakes increase. Equipment overheats. The heat is not a backdrop to the story. It is a structural constraint.

The same constraint applied globally. The tropics (which, btw, are home to the majority of the world's land area and a significant portion of its population) were historically disadvantaged in industrial production not solely because of colonialism (though colonialism was certainly doing a lot of heavy lifting) but also because the climate made sustained factory-floor work brutal. It is not a coincidence that the first Industrial Revolution happened in England, a country so committed to grey drizzle that its residents treat 22 degrees as a heatwave.

What Carrier Actually Built

Carrier's 1902 machine worked by passing air over chilled coils, dropping the temperature and causing moisture to condense and drain away. The refrigeration cycle it used was not new. It had been understood since the mid-19th century, and mechanical refrigeration had existed since the 1870s. What Carrier contributed was the systematic application of this principle to the problem of controlling air in large enclosed spaces, and then, crucially, the engineering work to make it reliable, scalable, and eventually cheap.

His 1906 patent Apparatus for Treating Air is one of the least dramatic documents in the history of civilisation relative to its consequences. It describes, in the arid language of engineering patents, a method for maintaining constant temperature and humidity in an enclosed space. What it describes, in practice, is the precondition for the modern office, the modern factory, the modern hospital, the modern server farm, and the modern shopping mall.

The commercial rollout was slow. Early systems were enormous, expensive, and primarily found in cinemas. This is why American cinema attendance peaked in the summer, because it was one of the few places you could reliably be cool. Department stores installed systems in the 1920s to attract customers. Factories followed. Offices followed. The residential unit arrived in the 1950s and prices dropped through the 1960s.

The Sun Belt and the Rewriting of American Power

The political consequences of this technology are almost comically large.

In 1950, the American political and economic centre of gravity was the Northeast and the Midwest. New York, Chicago, Detroit, Pittsburgh. The South was poor, agricultural, and, in the language of economists, a heat-constrained economy. Air conditioning changed this with a speed that no political scientist, economist, or urban planner had predicted.

Between 1950 and 2000, Florida's population grew by 800 percent. Phoenix grew from 100,000 people to 1.3 million. Las Vegas went from a desert outpost to one of the fastest-growing cities in American history. Houston became an energy and medical hub. The entire geography of American political power shifted south and west, and with it the culture, the politics, and the economics of the country.

Ronald Reagan was a California governor. Both Bush presidents were Texans. The evangelical political movement had its institutional base in the South. None of this is geographically possible without air conditioning. You cannot build a city of four million people in the Sonoran Desert without a reliable mechanism for keeping those people alive in July.

The implications for American conservatism alone are worth an entire academic career. The Sun Belt coalition that powered Republican politics from Nixon onward was, at its foundation, a demographic phenomenon made possible by a refrigeration technology.

The Global Factory Floor

The second great transformation powered by cooling is the industrialisation of the tropics.

Singapore is perhaps the most extreme case. Lee Kuan Yew was entirely explicit about this. In his memoirs, he described air conditioning as the most important invention of the 20th century because it allowed people in the tropics to work at the same efficiency as people in temperate climates. Singapore in 1965 was a small, resource-poor city-state in equatorial Southeast Asia. By 2000 it had one of the highest per capita incomes in the world.

The same logic applies to the Gulf States. Abu Dhabi's average July temperature is 42 degrees. Without mechanical cooling, the modern city that stands there with its glass towers, its financial institutions, its universities and hospitals, is physically impossible. The oil wealth enabled the infrastructure. The air conditioning made the infrastructure livable.

Across Southeast Asia, manufacturing export zones run in climates that would have made sustained factory-floor work brutal in the pre-cooling era. The precision manufacturing that has made the region central to global supply chains requires not just cool workers but cool machines. A chip fabrication plant is essentially an air conditioning problem with some semiconductor physics attached.

The Cost Nobody Talks About

Here is where the story becomes genuinely uncomfortable.

Air conditioning is one of the largest contributors to global electricity demand and, by extension, carbon emissions. The International Energy Agency estimates that the number of air conditioning units in operation globally will triple by 2050, adding demand equivalent to the current electricity consumption of the United States and Europe combined.

The feedback loop is not subtle. A warmer climate requires more cooling. More cooling requires more energy. More energy, if it comes from fossil fuels, produces more warming. The machine that allowed humanity to escape the constraints of heat is now, at scale, contributing to making those constraints worse.

This is not an argument against air conditioning. It is an argument for understanding that every transformative technology carries its consequences inside it, and that the bill for a century of cool offices and liveable cities in the tropics is now arriving.

The Unglamorous Point

Air conditioning does not appear in most histories of the 20th century. It is not glamorous. It hums. It drips. It makes a faint clicking noise when it turns off.

But it is the reason you can run a call centre in Chennai, build semiconductors in Malaysia, work in an office in Riyadh, live in Phoenix, and have surgery in a sterile room anywhere in the world. It is the reason the global economy looks the way it does, is distributed the way it is, and is as productive as it is.

Willis Carrier set out to stop ink from smearing. He ended up reshaping the geography of human civilisation.

That is, by any reasonable measure, a good return on a humidity problem.
