Shipping containers - much ado about nothing?

Said nobody ever in the shipping world.

Shree Solanki  ·  4 June 2025  ·  7 min read

The Box That Built the World

The Unglamorous Revolution, No. 2 of 10

On April 26, 1956, a crane lifted 58 metal boxes onto a converted tanker called the Ideal X in the port of Newark, New Jersey. The ship sailed to Houston. The boxes were unloaded. Nobody wrote anything about it. Nobody called it historic. A journalist covering the port that day would have found the scene boring.

Those 58 boxes were the first containerised cargo shipment in history. Within 50 years, they had restructured global manufacturing, made modern consumer culture possible, turned China into the world's factory, and created supply chains so efficient and so fragile that a single ship stuck sideways in a canal could hold up billions of dollars of global trade.

The shipping container is a metal box. It is also, without meaningful exaggeration, the physical infrastructure of globalisation.

Why Moving Things Was Expensive

To understand what the container changed, you need to understand what loading a ship looked like before 1956.

It was called break-bulk shipping, and it was exactly as inefficient as the name suggests. Cargo arrived at the port in every conceivable form: crates, barrels, bags, loose items, bundles tied with rope. Longshoremen (dock workers) unloaded it from trucks and warehouses, carried it across the pier, and loaded it piece by piece into the hold of a ship, where other workers arranged it to use space efficiently and prevent shifting at sea. This process was repeated in reverse at the destination.

A ship might spend as much time in port being loaded and unloaded as it does at sea. Labour costs for handling cargo were, in many cases, higher than the cost of the voyage itself. Theft was endemic because a crate that passed through dozens of hands across a single journey was a crate that reliably arrived lighter than it left. Damage was routine. Paperwork was staggering.

In the early 1950s, moving a ton of cargo from one American port to another cost approximately $5.83 in handling charges. This does not sound like much until you consider that it applies to every single item in every single shipment on every single voyage, and that the handling charges often exceeded the value of what was being shipped.

Malcolm McLean's Extremely Practical Idea

Malcolm McLean (they have got the name to win the game, that's for sure) was a trucking company owner from North Carolina who spent years watching trucks queue at the port of New York waiting to unload cargo onto ships. Each truck waited hours. The cargo was handled multiple times. The process was, to his eye, very obviously stupid.

His insight was simple: you did not unload the truck at all. You simply lifted the cargo compartment off the truck and put it directly on the ship; you would eliminate most of the handling, most of the labour, and most of the cost. The cargo would be loaded once, at the point of origin, and unloaded once, at the final destination. Everything in between would be mechanised.

(Look, in hindsight, it sounds insane that it was a long time between shipping on the sea started and good sir McClean brought this idea into fruition. But please consider unions, policies, and sheer corporate greed before you say old-timey folks were slow. Don't take my word for it. Read Peter S. Goodman's How The World Ran Out of Everything.)

The idea required a standardised box that could be lifted by cranes, stacked on ships, transferred to trains, and placed on trucks - all without opening it. It required ports to invest in cranes and new infrastructure. It required ships to be redesigned or rebuilt. It required, most importantly, an agreement on the dimensions of the box so that one manufacturer's container would fit on another company's ship.

That last part took longer than the engineering. American standards were agreed in the late 1950s. International standards, negotiated through the International Organisation for Standardisation, were settled in 1968. The standard twenty-foot container (the famous TEU, or Twenty-foot Equivalent Unit, which is still the measure of port capacity worldwide) became the common denominator of global trade.

What Happened Next

The cost of shipping cargo dropped by approximately 97% between 1956 and the 1970s.

Read that again. Ninety-seven percent.

The consequences cascaded through the global economy in ways that took decades to fully play out. The most visible was the transformation of manufacturing geography.

Before containerisation, manufacturing happened close to where goods were consumed, because transport costs made it expensive to do otherwise. A factory in the American Midwest made sense because it was near its customers, and the cost of shipping finished goods across an ocean to a foreign market was prohibitive. Labour costs mattered, but they mattered in the context of transport costs that were large enough to offset wage differentials.

Once transport costs collapsed, geography became largely irrelevant. A factory in Shenzhen producing electronics for consumers in Germany suddenly made economic sense in a way it simply had not before. The wage differential between a Chinese worker and a German worker was enormous. The cost of putting the finished product on a container ship and sailing it to Hamburg was trivial. The calculation that had anchored manufacturing to wealthy consumer markets for a century dissolved almost overnight.

This is the mechanism behind the deindustrialisation of the American Midwest and the British Midlands, the rise of Chinese manufacturing, the emergence of global supply chains, and the consumer price environment of the late 20th century. Everything became cheaper. Everything also became more connected, and therefore more fragile.

The Supply Chain as Infrastructure

Modern supply chains are an engineering achievement that receives essentially no public recognition. The iPhone you are possibly reading this on contains components manufactured in 43 countries. The semiconductors come from Taiwan. The rare earth elements come from Congo and China. The screens come from South Korea. The assembly happens in China. The logistics moving all of these pieces to their intermediate and final destinations are coordinated by systems of extraordinary complexity, running on schedules measured in days and hours rather than weeks and months.

None of this is possible without the container. The container makes it economical to move partial components across oceans multiple times during a production process. It makes just-in-time manufacturing possible, because the reliability and cost of shipping allow companies to treat distant suppliers as effectively local. It allows a company to hold almost no inventory because goods are always in transit, always arriving on schedule, always available.

Until they are not.

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed what supply chain professionals had known and executives had mostly ignored: that the efficiency of the containerised global supply chain was purchased at the cost of resilience. When the system was disrupted by port closures, labour shortages, and the Ever Given blocking the Suez Canal for six days in March 2021, the consequences were not local. They were global. Semiconductor shortages shut down car factories in Germany. Furniture ordered in March arrived in November. The price of a shipping container from China to Europe rose by 1,000 percent in under a year.

The box had made the world one factory floor. When the factory floor had a problem, the world had a problem.

The Unglamorous Point

The shipping container is not an invention. It is a standardisation. It is the agreement that a box should be 20 feet long, 8 feet wide, and 8.5 feet tall, and that every crane, ship, truck, and train in the world should be built to handle that box.

The lesson is not about engineering. It is about coordination. The container worked not because it was a technological marvel. The technology was pretty straightforward, but because enough people agreed to use the same dimensions and the roadblocks of beauracracy moved it worked. Standards, it turns out, are enablers of inventions.

The global economy is, at its foundation, a set of agreed-upon boxes. That is either deeply reassuring or deeply alarming, depending on your tolerance for the fragility of consensus.
