Book Review

Demon Copperhead

Barbara Kingsolver · 2022

★★★★★

Fine, You Were Right. I Loved It.

*Demon Copperhead* by Barbara Kingsolver | Harper Collins, 2022 | 560 pp.

Let me begin with a confession: I did not pick this book up voluntarily. I was bullied into it, not by a person, but by the internet's collective book-nerd conscience, that relentless chorus of "you HAVE to read this" and five-star reviews with crying emojis in the caption. I resisted for longer than I am proud of. A 560-page Pulitzer Prize-winning retelling of *David Copperfield* set in the Appalachian opioid crisis did not, on paper, scream *fun Friday night*, which is my only defence.

Reader, I was wrong. Reader, I am eating humble pie. And gleefully I must say, dearest Reader, it is delicious.

What It Actually Is

Damon Fields (called Demon Copperhead by everyone who matters) is born in a single-wide trailer in southern Virginia to a teenage mother whose primary expertise is cycling through rehab. His father is dead. His hair is copper red. His situation is, from page one, spectacularly not good. What follows is 560 pages of Kingsolver tracking this boy through foster homes, football fields, pill mills, first loves, and the particular kind of grinding poverty that America has perfected the art of not looking at directly.

The Dickens parallel is NOT subtle (Kingsolver all but winks at you through the acknowledgements, good god), but the reimagining is genuinely inspired. Victorian England's institutional cruelty maps onto 1990s Appalachia with an accuracy that is more damning than any op-ed. Where Dickens had debtors' prisons and child labour, Kingsolver has the foster care system and OxyContin. Where he had factory owners, she has pharmaceutical executives. Different centuries, identical arithmetic: extract everything from people with nothing, then blame them for ending up with less.

What saves this from being a lecture is Demon himself. He is one of the most alive narrators in recent American fiction. Funny, sharp, furious in the way only someone who has been failed repeatedly by adults can be furious (which is to say, not with self-pity but with a wised-up, clear-eyed contempt that never tips into bitterness). You spend 560 pages inside his head, and it is never once a chore, and never once did I look to the door to leave. Kingsolver gives him a voice so specific, so inhabited, that you forget she's a middle-aged woman who grew up in Kentucky writing him. You forget she's writing him at all.

The Part That Works Better Than It Should

The opioid crisis sections are the book's gut-punch and its backbone. Kingsolver does not explain the crisis to you. Instead, she puts you inside it, which is, by the way, a far more uncomfortable thing. Demon's descent into addiction follows a football injury and a prescription, two facts that alone constitute an indictment of an entire system. The chemistry of dependence is rendered without melodrama. There is no single moment of moral failure, no dramatic bad decision. There is just the slow, almost administrative way a body starts to need something it didn't need before, and the almost administrative way every structure around it fails to help.

The premise on pure technicality is harrowing and occasionally miserable. But bear with me, Reader, for saying it's also, somehow, propulsive. You don't read this book despite its darkness. You read it because Kingsolver has worked out exactly how much light to let in at exactly the right moments. Demon's friendship with Angus. His relationship with his art. The dry, dark humour that runs through everything like a thread you can grip when the current gets strong.

The One Thing That Creaks

Here is my small, slightly nervous criticism, offered respectfully to a Pulitzer Prize winner: the Dickens scaffolding, brilliant as the concept is, occasionally shows through the walls.

There are moments when a character arrives a little too neatly, when you can feel the authorial hand placing them precisely where their counterpart stood in *David Copperfield*, and the seams of the construction (which it has 99% of the time never felt like it) are briefly, distractingly visible. Demon's "Steerforth" analogue, his "Agnes," his "Murdstone" - when these figures appear, the novel sometimes pauses its own momentum to honour the correspondence. For readers who haven't read Dickens, these moments land just fine. For those who have (I have because my childhood was spent gallivanting on the internet, that was largely American), there is occasional friction between following Demon's story and watching Kingsolver manage her source material. The two tasks are almost always in harmony. But not always.

It is a minor thing. A structural chink in an otherwise masterfully built house. I raise it only because this review would be dishonest without it. (Humblest apologies to everyone reading this. This book is brilliant. The laurels it earns are deserved, and my title to critique it is non-existent, in all honesty, so a pinch of salt to go with it all is suggested, dearest Reader.)

What It Leaves You With

*Demon Copperhead* is angry at the right things and tender toward the right people, which I think is difficult to pull off at this length without becoming a thesis with characters stapled to it. Kingsolver never loses sight of the fact that she is writing a boy, not a rallying figure to pin up the messes of the world. The acknowledgement that Demon's suffering belongs to him and not to her.

The book has been praised for giving voice to Appalachia, for humanising a community that American media alternately ignores and caricatures. This is true and worth saying. But what I didn't expect, going in with my arms folded and my scepticism intact, was how much I would simply enjoy it. How often I would laugh. How badly I would want things to go right for this kid. How strange it would feel, 560 pages later, to no longer be inside his head.

The internet bookworms were right! I am now one!
