Book Review

How the World Ran Out of Everything: Inside the Global Supply Chain

Peter S. Goodman · 2024

★★★★☆

The Right Diagnosis, the Wrong Prescription

*How the World Ran Out of Everything: Inside the Global Supply Chain* by Peter S. Goodman | Mariner Books, 2024 | 416 pp.

TL;DR - Peter S. Goodman's *How the World Ran Out of Everything* is vivid, morally serious, and deeply reported. It is also, at its prescriptive core, a work that mistakes a compelling story for a complete argument. Read it. But read it with your sceptical hat on.

The Story Nobody Told This Well

Goodman has spent years circling the machinery of global trade. Here, he descends into its guts. And the view from the inside is not pretty.

The organizing conceit is elegant: a single product, a glow-in-the-dark bath toy called the Glo, becomes the thread through which he unravels the entire global supply chain's collapse. An American entrepreneur, manufacturing in China, scrambling to get his holiday shipment across the Pacific in the chaos of autumn 2021, becomes a kind of Odysseus navigating a broken world. It works brilliantly.

Goodman moves through the book's three parts: the disruption, the infrastructure, and the reckoning. He does so with the confidence of a journalist who has actually done the miles. He rides with long-haul truckers across the Great Plains. He visits seafarers trapped aboard ships for months, unable to disembark, ghost workers keeping civilisation's circulatory system flowing while the world argued about vaccines. He walks slaughterhouse floors and sits with almond farmers in California. Humanity isn't decorative. It is the argument. Every worker he profiles is evidence that "efficiency," as practised by the global supply chain, had a hidden price tag that never showed up on any spreadsheet.

His central thesis is sound: decades of just-in-time manufacturing, pursued with ideological zeal by consultants who treated efficiency as the only worthy god, left global supply chains catastrophically brittle. When COVID hit, the system didn't bend. It snapped. Shelves emptied. Ports seized. Container ships queued for weeks. Parents couldn't find baby formula. Carmakers couldn't find chips the size of a thumbnail. The wealthiest country on earth ran short of basic protective gear in the middle of a public health emergency. Goodman is right to call this a scandal and right to trace its roots back to the boardroom decisions of the 1980s and 1990s, when inventory was reframed as waste and supply chain concentration was reframed as sophistication.

He is particularly sharp on American complicity. His line lands like a verdict: *"It was not China that was ransacking American sustenance. If a crime had been committed, it was an inside job."* The rush to offshore manufacturing was not coercion; it was a choice, made by executives chasing quarterly margins, celebrated by business schools that confused efficiency with intelligence.

For a 416-page book about logistics, it never drags. That in itself is quite an achievement.

Where the Argument Frays

By the final section, something shifts. Goodman, the reporter, becomes Goodman the reformer, and the book begins to strain under the weight of its own certainties. His prescriptions to localise supply chains, hold more inventory, and return to a postwar model of industrial governance arrive with great posture. But here, the confidence isn't quite earned like before.

Start with inventory. Goodman treats just-in-time manufacturing as a reckless gamble. The pandemic exposed that very gamble. But "just store more" is not the corrective it sounds like. A 2022 SAP survey of 400 supply chain decision-makers found that nearly two-thirds were already shifting from just-in-time to just-in-case strategies in the immediate aftermath of COVID. What followed? Massive inventory gluts in 2022 and 2023. Retailers who had overstocked found themselves sitting on billions in dead capital, running emergency markdowns, and booking losses. The very companies that "fixed" the problem by holding more inventory got punished for it a year later. This is not a paradox; it is precisely what supply chain researchers at Taylor & Francis have been documenting: "proactively investing in inventory can fortify resilience through redundancy but at the same time increase inventory holding costs." Getting the balance wrong in either direction is expensive. There is no free lunch in the warehouse.

The localisation argument is thornier still. Goodman is correct that concentration in a single geography is a fragility. But the leap from "too concentrated" to "produce locally" glosses over complications the book largely waves past. Research published in the Springer Journal of Industrial and Business Economics makes the point clearly: reshoring is not simply the reverse of offshoring. It carries its own substantial fixed costs, such as establishing local supplier networks, reorganising production, and rebuilding contract enforcement infrastructure. And these don't disappear because the politics are appealing. Apple still manufactures iPhones in Asia, not because its executives are unaware of supply chain risk, but because the industrial ecosystem of precision tooling, component supply chains, and skilled assembly workforce does not exist at scale anywhere else. You cannot legislate your way into creating an ecosystem within a decade.

The University of Arkansas supply chain policy group put this plainly when evaluating U.S. government resilience mandates: simply encouraging firms to hold more safety stock and add manufacturing capacity could "stifle innovation, produce unnecessary waste, and limit companies' ability to pursue environmental and social sustainability goals." Resilience built by brute-force stockpiling ties up capital that could otherwise fund R&D, automation, or the very workforce improvements Goodman champions elsewhere in the same book.

None of this means localisation is wrong as a direction. The IMD Business School's research on reshoring is more nuanced than Goodman's advocacy: it argues localisation makes good business sense *when total acquisition costs (packaging, transportation, customs, tariffs, exchange rate insurance) rise to the point of competitive disadvantage*. That is a calculation, not a slogan. The answer varies by industry, by product, by geopolitical movement. A chip foundry and a glow-in-the-dark bath toy do not have the same optimal supply chain architecture.

Then there's the historical reach. Goodman's call to return to "the mode of governance that prevailed in the United States from the end of World War II through the late 1970s" romanticises a period whose external conditions were favourable (such as Europe rebuilding from rubble, no serious industrial competition from Asia, and cheap domestic energy), and simply cannot be recreated by policy choice. That world isn't available for re-import.

What the Book Actually Proves

These are my criticisms, but they should not obscure what Goodman has actually pulled off, which is beyond impressive. He has written the definitive popular account of how supply chain logic became supply chain theology. How the consultant class convinced corporate America that resilience was merely inefficiency in disguise, and how governments let it happen because the quarterly numbers looked good. The human beings he profiles: the seafarer, the trucker, the slaughterhouse worker, they are not embellishments to his story; they are the reason this book is so important. They are the manifestation of the externalities that the efficiency models never once accounted for.

He is also right that the pandemic revealed a political failure, not just a corporate one. Governments that had outsourced industrial strategy to markets, and then to the consultants who served those markets, found themselves with no levers to pull when the crisis came. That observation stands and deserves to be louder.

The book's limitation is not its reporting, which is exemplary. It is in its confidence that the diagnosis implies the cure. Supply chain resilience is genuinely, stubbornly hard. It is a field of trade-offs where every gain in robustness carries a cost in efficiency, every buffer stock is capital on ice, and every domestic factory is a subsidy paid somewhere downstream. The right response to COVID's lessons is not to undo globalisation but to build more intelligent redundancy by having diversified sourcing, strategic reserves for critical goods, serious logistics infrastructure investment and all the while accepting that some concentration and some efficiency will remain the rational choice for most products, most of the time.

All of this is not to discredit how genuinely interesting this book is. How genuinely necessary this book's humanity is. So the final verdict is - regardless of who you are and what interests you, please read it!
