Book Review

Why I Am a Hindu

Shashi Tharoor · 2018

★★★☆☆

A Book About My Religion That Wasn't Quite About Mine

Why I Am a Hindu by Shashi Tharoor | Aleph Book Company, 2018 | 294 pp.

I should tell you upfront what kind of Hindu I am, so you know what kind of reader I brought to this book.

I don't fast. I don't observe the pious months. I can't tell you which deity governs which Tuesday. My kinship with this religion was not, in the beginning, something I chose. Honestly, it was ambient, inherited, pressed into me by a household that observed it with a seriousness I found more suffocating than sacred. What I have now, in my adult life, looks quite different: a small quiet temple, an unhurried offering, a hope for the well-being of the people I love. No priest required. No calendar consulted. Something that feels, finally, mine.

This is the Hinduism I showed up with when I opened Shashi Tharoor's Why I Am a Hindu. And Tharoor's Hinduism, it turns out, is not quite the same thing. This makes this book genuinely interesting to review. It has been more than I expected. But it has also been less than what I needed it to be.

What He Is Arguing

The book moves in three acts: Tharoor's personal faith, his anatomy of political Hinduism, and his case for taking the religion back from the forces distorting it.

The personal section is the most disarming. Tharoor (parliamentarian, former UN diplomat, and man who visibly enjoys being the most eloquent person in any room) writes about belief with surprising plainness. He describes a religion without a single founder, a single holy text, a single mandatory creed. A faith less like a doctrine and more like a civilisation: an open-ended conversation running for four thousand years, in which no position is finally heretical because no single truth is finally unchallengeable. His anchor is the Rig Veda: ekam sat vipra bahudha vadanti i.e., truth is one, but the wise call it by many names. This, he argues, is not a bumper sticker. It is the chassis of the entire tradition.

The argument is genuinely illuminating, especially for anyone who has spent time trying to explain Hinduism to someone raised in an Abrahamic faith and found all the usual frameworks breaking down. Tharoor gives you the vocabulary for why they break down. That is useful.

It is also, from the first chapter, hard going.

The Readability Problem

Tharoor name-drops schools of philosophy, ancient texts, and Sanskrit concepts the way a cricket commentator name-drops statistics- fluently, confidently, and with the serene assumption that you are keeping up. Advaita Vedanta, Dvaita, Vishishtadvaita. The Mimamsa school. The Nyaya tradition. These arrive in quick succession with minimal scaffolding, each one a door you are expected to already have a key to.

For readers steeped in Hindu philosophy, this is probably bracing. For the rest of us — including, I suspect, a significant portion of the Indian readers this book most needs to reach — it means a second device open to Wikipedia at all times and the occasional humbling experience of reading a paragraph three times before it releases its meaning.

The prose itself, while always precise, settles into an academic register that can feel, over hundreds of pages, more like organized testimony than propulsive argument.

Where It Gets Urgent

The second half, on political Hinduism, is where the book earns its existence.

Tharoor's central argument against Hindutva is not that it is un-Indian (though he pointedly makes that case too) but that it is un-Hindu. That the ideology built by nationalist movements around Hindu identity represents a betrayal of what Hinduism, by its own internal logic, actually is. Hindutva is a 20th-century political construction that borrows religious language while gutting religious content: replacing pluralism with exclusion, philosophical openness with manufactured resentment, the tradition's historic ease with ambiguity with the anxious need for an enemy.

This distinction matters and he makes it with care and genuine force. I found myself agreeing with most of it. I also found myself wanting him to go further.

What the Book Doesn't Say

Here is where I part ways with Tharoor, respectfully but clearly.

The Hinduism he defends: pluralistic, philosophically generous, and historically hospitable to Jews, Zoroastrians, and Buddhists. It is real. It exists. It is also incomplete as a portrait of the religion as actually practised, across centuries, by actual people. And a book called Why I Am a Hindu that does not reckon seriously with caste feels, to me, like a significant omission.

Caste is not incidental to Hinduism. It is not an aberration that crept in from outside and corrupted an otherwise beautiful tradition. It is woven into the Manusmriti. It structured temple access, well access, the right to learn Sanskrit, and the right to be cremated with full rites. Untouchability was not invented by politicians - it was sanctified by scripture and enforced by priests. Women's subordination - their exclusion from certain rituals, their reduction in many texts to vehicles for male lineage - is not a historical footnote. It shaped, and in many places continues to shape, the lived experience of being Hindu. Shashi Tharoor kindly says that these are not the versions of Hinduism that we should keep in mind in today's world, but take back what it means to be Hindu by being secular. But to Shashi Tharoor, I must ask: I grew up watching the lines of casteism, elitism, and misogynistic-rooted separatism thrive under the name of Hinduism, so which one is Hinduism? The one you saw practices, the one I saw practised, or the one the world has made into a weapon of political will?

In Shashi Tharoor's defense he does addresses caste briefly. He acknowledges the reformers - Ambedkar, the Bhakti saints, Vivekananda - who pushed back against it. But acknowledgement is not reckoning. You cannot spend two hundred pages celebrating Hinduism's radical openness and then give three paragraphs to the most systematic exclusion the tradition produced. It creates a portrait that is technically accurate and even my relatively sheltered living as a Kshatriya experientially incomplete. It creates a pristine, perfect image of the Hinduism of the philosophical elite, not the Hinduism of those the philosophy was used to exclude.

This is the version of "we are amazing, mostly" that I cannot entirely accept. The tradition's beauty is real. So is its violence. Both deserve to be in the room.

Where That Leaves Me

I came to this book as someone who had spent years untangling my own relationship with a religion I was handed rather than chosen, and pulling out the parts that felt like mine and setting aside the parts that felt like someone else's rules dressed in devotional clothing. I was hoping Tharoor might make that process easier, might give me language for the Hinduism I was trying to inhabit.

He does, partly. The idea of a faith that has no mandatory beliefs, no single correct path, that accommodates doubt as naturally as devotion has genuinely helped. It named something I had been practicing without having words for. The small quiet temple, the unscripted prayer, the absence of a priest between me and whatever I'm addressing. It turns out there are four thousand years of philosophical tradition that says this is, in fact, enough.

What he doesn't do is help me with the parts of the tradition that hurt people. And for a religion with Hinduism's history, that feels like a necessary conversation to have alongside the defence.

Why I Am a Hindu is worth reading. It is important at this particular political moment, and Tharoor is right about the things that matter most. But it is a defence of Hinduism's ceiling, written by someone standing comfortably in its upper floors. A fuller book would have found its way to the crooked basement and skeletons in its closet, too.
